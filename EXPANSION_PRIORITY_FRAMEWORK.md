# Expansion Priority Framework

This document defines how the wider `lippytm` ecosystem can prioritize future expansion across education, AI development, cybersecurity, sandbox systems, reusable products, memberships, affiliates, and community participation.

## Purpose
Make continued growth easier to manage by clarifying which kinds of expansion should usually come first, which should follow, and how the ecosystem can stay balanced while growing.

## Core Principle
Expand in layers: strengthen foundations first, then deepen teaching and support systems, then broaden distribution, community, and specialization.

## Priority Layers

### 1. Foundation Priority
Examples:
- repo role clarity
- setup notes
- review loops
- security notes
- toolkit and sandbox guidance

Why it matters:
- weak foundations multiply confusion when cloned or adapted

### 2. Learning Priority
Examples:
- curriculum maps
- lesson kits
- teaching loops
- builder review loops
- mixed-environment guidance

Why it matters:
- strong learning systems make every later expansion more reusable

### 3. Workflow Priority
Examples:
- secure agent workflows
- agent training patterns
- workflow sandboxes
- operational runbooks

Why it matters:
- repeatable workflows turn theory into practical systems

### 4. Productization Priority
Examples:
- educational kits
- starter systems
- content bundles
- release patterns
- support cycles

Why it matters:
- productization makes the ecosystem distributable and sustainable

### 5. Distribution Priority
Examples:
- memberships
- affiliates
- product variants
- audience-specific content systems

Why it matters:
- distribution grows reach once the system is teachable and maintainable

### 6. Community Priority
Examples:
- contributor pathways
- partner participation
- member participation
- collaborative improvements

Why it matters:
- broader participation works best after strong reusable systems exist

## Best Practices
- expand from stable bases instead of constant reinvention
- keep each priority layer documented before multiplying variants
- use support and review loops to decide what should scale next
- archive weak branches instead of keeping everything active forever
- promote strong repeated patterns into templates
